Sky High

Thia is a 3D physics stacking game. You drag shapes from a tray and stack them as high as you can until the tower topples. The most interesting design decision was the risk-reward scoring, the player is rewarded for choosing unstable shapes over stable ones. The player also gets extra points for having a variet of shapes in their stack instead of the same shape. These scoring rules were put in place to change how people play. Instead of always picking the safest shapes, players take risks as the rewards them for it.

Genre: Physics, arcade

Engine: Unity

Team: Solo

My roles: Game Designer, Programmer

Status:Released

Read the Design Report

Design goals

The core loop is risky stacking. I used Garneau's 14 Forms of Fun to pick the three types of fun the game is built on and every design decision serves them.

  • Application of an Ability: The player can choose where to drop the shape so they must precisely drag and drop shape to land neatly with the margin for error shrinking as the stack grows taller.
  • Thrill of Danger: Every placement carries a risk. The thin pieces are unstable and placing one makes it more likely the whole stack will topple.
  • Intellectual Problem Solving: The player must decide what shape to place and when. The player is always thinking of risk vs reward.

Risk-reward scoring

Result: Players stopped picking the safest shape each time and took risks it became the best way to get a higher score.

Problem: When stacking items the most obvious thing to do is to place the biggest and most stable shape repeatedly. This isn't interesting and the game didn't do anyhting to punish this.

Solution: I made the unstable shapes worth more points than stable ones and placing a thin piece low in the stack doubles its score if it stays balanced. Now each placement will have players thinking between safety and maxing their score.

Variety bonus

Result: Towers stopped being made of just one shape and were much more varied, it made each run play differently as players try new shapes and have to learn the different physics when stacking them.

Problem: Even with a risk-reward scoring implemented a player could still find one shape they were good at balancing and just use that. The game didn't rewarded players from using a variety of shapes.

Solution: The player earns a bonus the first time each new shape appears in the stack. So now a great way to increase your score is by trying to use every shape in the tray and finding a way to including the unstable ones too.

Iteration

Early builds were completely grey with the default Unity skybox and a grey floor. This was my first time making a 3D game. It worked but it looked unfinished. I added materials in Unity and coloured the floor brown and the background yellow. This way the game feels like it takes place in a room. I added a tray at the side of the screen with each shape being a different colour. As my first 3D game the materials applied in Unity made a big difference visually for very little work.

What I learned

I learned that spending time on perfecting the scoring system did more for the game than any physics improvements could have. I'd keep the approach of designing rules that fight players making boring choices. Next time I'd spend a lot more time on learning 3D modelling to have a more ploished looking game.
