Ship It!

Ship It! is an arcade game where you sort items off a conveyor belt into bins before time runs out. Each round lasts 60 seconds, and the spawn rate ramps as the round goes on, so the opening seconds teach you the game and the final stretch tests how well you sort under pressure. It's a 2D web game built in Unity.

Genre: Arcade, score attack

Engine: Unity (2D, WebGL)

Team: Solo

My roles: Game Designer, Programmer, Artist

Status: Playable

Read the design report

Design goals

The core loop is built around quick decision making rather than precision. I used Garneau's 14 Forms of Fun to pick the three types of fun the game is built on, and every design decision serves them.

  • Application of Skill: The game tests fast recognition and accurate placement under time pressure. Item variety, bin count and conveyor speed all all create a challenge for the player without it being unfair.
  • Competition: The player competes against their own best score and the timer, not another player.
  • Advancement and Completion: Each run is short and ends in a clear result. When the player finishes and sees their score they will want to try beat it which is what gives the game its replayability.

Ramping spawn rate

Result: The round teaches itself. New players learn the sorting mechanic early on and and by the final stretch every player is sorting flat out under pressure.

Problem: At one stage I had the items spawning at the same rate. I tried to find one rate that worked for the whole game but sometimes it would be too fast at the start of the game or too slow by the end.

Solution: The spawn rate increases over the course of the round. Early on items spawn slowly so the player can learn how to sort them. By the halfway point the player has to commit to sorting decisions quickly and as the game finishes up, the spawn rate peaks.

Scoring that punishes spamming

Result: Players make real sorting decisions instead of dumping items into the nearest bin to clear the belt.

Problem: Under time pressure, players may be tempted to grab everything and throw into the closest bin. If there wasn't a penalty to this then that strategy might work.

Solution: Putting an item in the correct bin is worth 10 points, but putting it in the wrong bin costs the player 5 points and an item reaching the end of the belt costs the player 3 points. A wrong guess costs more than letting an item go. So spamming to clear the belt is never the safe option.

The 60 second round

Result: Every session is short, ends in a clear result and is tempting to play again.

Problem: The round needed to be long enough for a player to notice their own mistakes and improve within a single run. It also needed to be short enough that you could play a few rounds at once.

Solution: I capped the round at 60 seconds. Combined with the ramping spawn rate I believe that's enough time for a full playthrough.

Iteration

For the character sprite I hand drew each frame, then tore it out. I wanted it to have a scrapbook appearance, with the background in a rustic pixel art style behind it. The torn-paper effect doesn't come across as clear as I wanted it to as I underesitmated the sprite size on screen, so it's a lesson learned. The V1 design notes show where the game started, back when it still had a placeholder name.

After many playtests I did slow down the item spawning rate. As I felt it was impossible to keep up with. I was able to find a balance where it was still challenging but possible.

What I learned

The ramping spawn rate is the thing I'd keep. It teaches the player safely first while giving them a challenge later. The thing I'd do differently is testing art at actual gameplay size before committing to a style. The hand-torn sprite is one of my favourite parts of the project and nobody playing the game can see the details put into it.
